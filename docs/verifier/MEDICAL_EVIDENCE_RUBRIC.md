# Medical Evidence Rubric

## Purpose

This rubric defines how the agent should judge medical evidence before selecting topics, writing claims, or approving publication.

Use this rubric during:
- topic selection
- research synthesis
- writing
- validation

If a claim does not meet this rubric, downgrade it, qualify it, or omit it.

---

## Core Rule

**No material clinical claim should appear in a published article unless its evidence strength, limitations, and source traceability are clear.**

---

## Evidence Hierarchy

Rank evidence in this order whenever possible:

1. **Official guidance / public health authorities**
   - WHO, CDC, NIH, ECDC, national ministries of health
   - practice guidance, advisories, surveillance updates, safety alerts
2. **Peer-reviewed evidence syntheses or major guidelines**
   - systematic reviews, meta-analyses, consensus guidelines
3. **Peer-reviewed interventional studies**
   - randomized controlled trials, pragmatic trials
4. **Peer-reviewed observational studies / institutional reports**
   - cohort, case-control, registry, surveillance, hospital system reports
5. **Reputable medical journalism or summary reporting**
   - use for discovery or context, not as sole support for strong clinical claims

---

## Confidence Levels

Assign one confidence level to each major claim:

### High Confidence
- directly supported by official guidance or strong peer-reviewed evidence
- population, outcome, and effect are clearly defined
- limitations do not materially weaken the conclusion

### Moderate Confidence
- supported by credible evidence but not yet definitive
- may rely on one strong study, institutional data, or partial consensus
- should be written with measured language

### Low Confidence
- early signal, single-study result, indirect evidence, or weakly replicated finding
- must be labeled as preliminary or uncertain

### Mixed / Conflicting
- sources disagree or evidence points in multiple directions
- describe the disagreement explicitly or omit the claim

---

## Claim Handling Rules

### Allowed as confirmed
A claim may be written as established only if:
- it has high-confidence support, or
- it is an official recommendation, surveillance update, or clearly established fact

### Must be qualified
A claim must be written cautiously if:
- it is based on observational data only
- it comes from a single study
- it is preliminary, early, indirect, or not yet guideline-backed

Use language such as:
- "early evidence suggests"
- "observational data indicates"
- "preliminary findings reported"
- "officials stated"

### Must be omitted
Do not publish claims that are:
- unsupported by traceable sources
- based only on sensational headlines or summary articles with no primary basis
- internally contradictory without explanation
- missing essential context such as population, endpoint, timeframe, or comparator

---

## Prohibited Evidence Errors

Never do any of the following:
- turn correlation into causation
- turn a preprint into established fact
- turn a single study into universal guidance
- invent sample sizes, effect sizes, endpoints, or quotations
- infer safety or efficacy beyond what the source states
- generalize findings beyond the studied population without saying so
- convert journalistic framing into clinical recommendation

---

## Topic Selection Standard

Prefer topics that meet at least one of these conditions:
- backed by official public health guidance
- backed by a peer-reviewed study with direct clinical relevance
- materially affects diagnosis, treatment, safety, infection control, or health operations
- contains actionable implications for healthcare professionals

Reject or downgrade topics that are:
- trend-driven but weakly sourced
- based on unverified claims
- purely opinion or commentary
- medically interesting but operationally irrelevant to healthcare professionals

---

## Research Standard

For each topic, the research summary should separate:
- **Confirmed Findings**
- **Preliminary or Conflicting Findings**
- **Do-Not-Use Claims**

Each major confirmed claim should include:
- claim text
- evidence type
- confidence level
- source

---

## Writing Standard

The final article must:
- use only claims supported in the research summary
- preserve uncertainty where evidence is limited
- avoid overclaiming treatment benefit, safety, causality, or guideline status
- cite enough sources for a professional reader to trace the core claims

---

## Validation Gate

Validation should fail if any of the following are true:
- a major claim cannot be traced to evidence
- an observational finding is presented as proof of causation
- a statistic or recommendation is unsupported
- a preliminary finding is written as settled fact
- the sources are too weak for the strength of the claim

---

## Quick Decision Matrix

| Evidence situation | Publication handling |
|---|---|
| Official guidance or strong peer-reviewed support | May publish as confirmed |
| One credible study with limitations | Publish with caution and limitations |
| Early or conflicting evidence | Publish only with explicit uncertainty |
| Unsupported or unverifiable claim | Do not publish |

---

## Default Rule When Unsure

If you are unsure whether a claim is strong enough:

1. downgrade it to cautious language,
2. add limitations if they are known,
3. or omit it entirely.

When forced to choose between completeness and accuracy, choose accuracy.
